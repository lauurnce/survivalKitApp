## Unit 1: Understanding History Using Primary and Secondary Sources

---

### Lesson 1: The Meaning and Relevance of History

#### Why Study History?

History is more than a catalogue of dates and names. When studied properly, it moves past surface-level facts and asks the deeper questions: *Why did a certain event happen? How did it unfold?* These questions reveal how the past shaped the present and may shape the future.

The discipline of history serves several key purposes, identified by historian Peter Stearns (1998):

- **Moral understanding**, History presents humanity's ethical struggles, achievements, and failures, giving readers material to develop moral judgment.
- **Understanding people and societies**, Examining past behavior reveals patterns in how societies respond to crises, conflicts, and change.
- **Providing identity**, Communities understand who they are partly through their shared historical experiences.
- **Civic formation**, Informed citizenship depends on understanding how political systems, institutions, and rights were fought for and developed.

---

#### Where to Find Historical Sources

Historians don't work from imagination, they work from **sources**: the surviving records, objects, and testimonies of the past. Knowing where to locate them is a basic skill for any student of history.

**Philippine Repositories:**

The **National Library of the Philippines (NLP)** is the primary national depository, housing the Filipiniana Division and Microfilm Section. It holds crucial materials like the Historical Data Papers and Philippine Revolutionary Records.

The **National Archives of the Philippines (NAP)** holds government records from the Spanish colonial period. Other institutions include:

- The **Archives of the University of Santo Tomas**, rich in Spanish-era education records
- The **Archdiocesan Archives of Manila** (Intramuros), for ecclesiastical and church-related records
- The **SIL Philippines** (Quezon City), approximately 2,000 titles on Philippine languages
- Archives maintained by local historical networks and centers across various provinces

Several universities also maintain their own Filipiniana collections.

**Foreign Repositories:**

Because the Philippines spent over three centuries under Spanish rule, passed briefly through British hands (1762–1764), and later under American administration, significant Philippine historical records are scattered internationally:

| Country | Key Archives |
|---|---|
| Spain | Archivo General de Indias (Seville); Archivo Histórico Nacional, Real Academia de la Historia, Biblioteca Nacional de España (all in Madrid). The Spanish government's Portal de Archivos Españoles (PARES) offers free online access to many digitized documents. |
| Mexico | Archivo General de la Nación de México, relevant because the Philippines was under the Viceroyalty of New Spain until Mexico's independence in 1821. |
| United Kingdom | British Museum, some Spanish-period documents were taken during the British occupation of Manila (1762–64). |
| United States | National Archives and Records Service (NARS); Library of Congress; Ayer Collection (Newberry Library, Chicago); various university libraries. |

Online archives such as **archive.org** and **Project Gutenberg** offer a growing number of scanned primary sources freely available for download.

---

#### Classification and Types of Historical Sources

**Sources** are defined as artifacts left by the past. They fall into three broad categories: relics/remains, and testimonies.

An **artifact** is any object made or modified by humans for their own purposes, tools, artworks, pottery, documents. An **ecofact** is archaeological evidence from nature (bones, seeds, shells) that was not shaped by humans but still tells us about past environments and cultures.

#### Written Sources

**Primary Sources**

A primary source is the direct testimony of an eyewitness, someone who was present at the events being described, whether they recorded it through writing, photography, audio, or other means. Examples include diaries, letters, official documents (birth/marriage/death certificates), newspaper articles written at the time, photographs, and video recordings.

Historian Louis Gottschalk (1950) defines a primary source as "the testimony of an eyewitness, or of a witness by any other of the senses, or of a mechanical device... that is, of one who or that which was present at the events of which he or it tells."

**Secondary Sources**

A secondary source is produced by someone who was *not* present at the events, a historian writing about the past, an analyst interpreting earlier accounts, or a textbook summarizing documented events. Examples include bibliographies, encyclopedias, journal articles, monographs, and textbooks.

The key distinction between primary and secondary sources is **proximity to the event**. Primary sources come from inside the experience; secondary sources reflect on it from the outside.

#### Unwritten (Non-Written) Sources

Not all history is recorded in text. Three types of non-written evidence are commonly used:

- **Archaeological evidence**, Artifacts and ecofacts (tools, ornaments, ruins, agricultural implements) that reveal past cultures and lifeways.
- **Oral evidence**, Folk tales, myths, legends, folk songs, and rituals passed down through generations. These can reveal economic activities, social structures, and cultural values even when no written record exists.
- **Material evidence**, Photographs, artworks, videos, and sound recordings.

### Lesson 2: Tests of Authenticity and Credibility

#### Method vs. Historiography

Before examining any source, it helps to understand the difference between two related terms in historical practice:

**Historical Method** is the process of rigorously examining and critically analyzing the records and survivals of the past, applying a set of scientific rules to determine whether a reconstructed event actually occurred.

**Historiography** refers to the process of *writing* history, synthesizing tested data into a narrative, whether in a book, thesis, journal article, or conference paper. Before a historian begins writing, they must have completed the methodological work of examining their sources.

---

#### External Criticism: The Test of Authenticity

**Authenticity** means originality. The test of authenticity asks: *Is this source what it claims to be?*

This test is necessary because:

1. **Sources can be fabricated.** Some documents have been invented or forged to support false claims, either for personal, political, or national benefit. A historical example is the alleged *Maragtas*, a document that historian William Henry Scott (1984) argued was based on suspicious oral traditions and fabricated written sources rather than genuine pre-Hispanic records.

2. **Negative revisionism exists.** This refers to deliberate attempts to downplay, minimize, or distort historical events to reduce their perceived impact on certain individuals or institutions.

3. **Sources can mislead.** Misleading sources, even unintentionally, miseducate people by distorting the value or meaning of events.

In assessing authenticity, historians examine:

- **Authorship**, Who created the source? Knowing who produced it strengthens or weakens its reliability.
- **Time**, When was the event? When was the source created relative to that event?
- **Space (setting/context)**, Where did the event occur? A source must be situated in its proper context to be properly understood.

---

#### Internal Criticism: The Test of Credibility

Once authenticity is established, the historian turns to **internal criticism**, evaluating whether the source accurately represents reality.

For a source to be credible, Gottschalk (1950) suggests the historian assess:

1. The **competence** of the source in telling the truth (did the author have sufficient knowledge or vantage point?)
2. The **willingness** of the source to tell the truth (was there reason to deceive?)
3. The **adequacy** of the data, is the information detailed and complete enough?
4. The **reliability** of the source when tested against other independent sources

Gottschalk famously described the historian's role in assessing credibility as playing "prosecutor, attorney for the defense, judge, and jury all in one."

It is also important to recognize that sources carry **biases**, every author writes from a particular vantage point, with personal, cultural, or institutional interests that color their account. Identifying these biases does not automatically disqualify a source, but it shapes how the historian interprets it.

---

#### Basic Assumptions When Working with Sources

The following principles guide historians in evaluating primary sources:

1. Sources (relics, artifacts, documents, witnesses) are accurate when proven authentic and credible. Relics and artifacts tend to be more reliable; documents and testimonies are more detailed and specific.
2. Authenticity and credibility reinforce each other.
3. A primary source is generally more reliable than a secondary one.
4. Credibility increases when multiple independent sources agree.
5. Sources tend to carry biases toward their place of origin or the person/institution that produced them.
6. Witnesses without direct personal interest in an event tend to be more credible than those with a stake in it.
7. When all independent sources agree on an event, that event is generally accepted as factual.
8. Testimony is most credible when the witness was mentally and emotionally fit at the time of their account.
9. A source that does not fit the milieu (context) of its supposed period is likely fabricated.

---

#### Dealing with Disagreeing or Hostile Sources

When sources contradict each other:

1. If both are equally valid, prefer the one with more logical reasoning and common sense.
2. If authenticity and credibility are unequal, prefer the more solidly authenticated source.
3. Hostile sources (those with an adversarial perspective toward the subject) must be approached with extra caution and corroborated.
4. Sources representing a fixed ideological viewpoint should be cross-checked against others not aligned with that perspective.

---

#### Ethics in Historical Research

Historians are expected to uphold the following standards:

- Be aware of personal biases (point of view, prior assumptions, values)
- Remain objective and accurate in examining and analyzing sources
- Be impartial in interpretation and synthesis
- Use all relevant sources, including those that challenge your argument
- Avoid outside influence; subject yourself only to the truth
- Cite sources properly
- Avoid plagiarism, fabrication, deception, or academic dishonesty
- Acknowledge assistance and debts to other scholars
- Respect peer criticism
- Avoid irresponsible or distorted interpretation
- Never use sources to deliberately mislead or modify history for personal benefit

### Lesson 3: Tests of Authenticity and Credibility, Case Study: The Tejeros Convention of 1897

#### Background: The Katipunan Split

The Kataas-taasang Kagalang-galangang Katipunan ng mga Anak ng Bayan, commonly called the Katipunan, was a secret society founded by Andres Bonifacio to fight for Philippine independence from Spain. By 1896, the Katipunan had expanded rapidly and its internal structures, called Sangguniang Bayan, had grown into two major factions:

- **Magdiwang**, based in Noveleta, led by Mariano Alvarez
- **Magdalo**, based in Kawit, led by Baldomero Aguinaldo

Tensions grew between Bonifacio and Emilio Aguinaldo over strategy and leadership. Bonifacio favored decisions made by consensus among senior members; Aguinaldo believed in centralized command. A series of Spanish military defeats further eroded Bonifacio's standing, especially in Cavite, and factional rivalry intensified.

To resolve the conflict, the **Tejeros Convention** was held on March 22, 1897. Elections were held: Aguinaldo was elected President of the new revolutionary government, and Bonifacio was elected Director of the Interior. When Danielo Tirona challenged Bonifacio's qualification for the position he had been elected to, Bonifacio declared the proceedings null and void. He was later arrested, tried, and executed on charges of treason in Maragondon, Cavite, in May 1897.

The Tejeros Convention is significant to historians not only for its political outcomes but because it illustrates how different sources record the same event very differently depending on the author's position, loyalties, and purpose.

---

#### The Two Primary Sources Under Analysis

This lesson uses the Tejeros Convention as a case study for applying external and internal criticism to two contrasting accounts:

**Source 1: *Katipunan and the Revolution: Memoirs of a General* by Santiago V. Alvarez**

Santiago Alvarez (1872–?) was a member of the Magdiwang faction and a direct participant in the events of the revolution. He served as Captain-General of Magdiwang forces and was known among his peers for his bravery in battle. His memoirs were originally serialized in the Tagalog weekly *Sampagita* in the 1920s and later published in book form with an English translation by Paula Carolina Malay.

Because Alvarez was present at the Tejeros Convention and was a member of the Magdiwang (Bonifacio's closer allies), his account is a **primary source**, but one with clear potential biases stemming from his political loyalties and personal experience of events.

**Source 2: *The Revolt of the Masses: The Story of Bonifacio and the Katipunan* by Teodoro A. Agoncillo**

Teodoro Agoncillo (1912–1985) was one of the most prominent Filipino historians of the 20th century and is credited with pioneering a **nationalist historiography**, the view that Philippine history must be written from a Filipino perspective, centered on the experiences of ordinary Filipinos rather than colonial administrators. His book, first published in 1956, argues that the masses, not the elite *ilustrados*, made the revolution possible.

Because Agoncillo was not an eyewitness to the events he describes, his work is a **secondary source**. However, he drew on a wide range of primary documents and is considered a major scholarly reference.

#### Applying the Tests: A Framework for Comparison

When comparing both accounts of the Tejeros Convention, students should evaluate:

**External Criticism (Authenticity):**
- Brief description of each source (genre, form, original language, publication history)
- Competence of the author (direct participant vs. later scholar)
- Origin of the information (personal witness vs. documentary research)
- Date of composition relative to the events described

**Internal Criticism (Credibility):**
- What were the objectives of each author?
- Who were the key persons discussed in each account?
- What are the visible biases of each author?
- How do the accounts compare with each other and with other independent sources?

### Lesson 1: Chronicles of 16th-Century Filipinos, The Magellan Expedition and the Customs of the Tagalogs

#### The Age of Exploration and the Philippine Encounter

The period commonly called the Age of Exploration (roughly the 15th and 16th centuries) marked a turning point for the Philippines. In 1521, Ferdinand Magellan's expedition inadvertently reached the Philippine archipelago, setting off a chain of subsequent voyages that would ultimately result in the conquest by Miguel López de Legazpi beginning in 1564–1565. Legazpi's arrival in Cebu effectively began over three centuries of Spanish colonial rule.

This lesson examines two chroniclers whose accounts are essential primary sources for understanding pre-colonial and early-contact Filipino society.

---

#### Antonio Pigafetta and the First Voyage Around the World

**Background on Pigafetta**

Antonio Pigafetta (c. 1491–1531) was an Italian scholar and explorer from Vicenza who became the official chronicler of the Magellan-Elcano expedition. He was among only 18 of the roughly 270 original crew members who returned to Spain, making his account extraordinarily rare.

His account is important to scholars for two key reasons: it is a **primary source** (he was aboard the expedition), and it remains the most detailed surviving firsthand narrative of the first circumnavigation of the globe. By contrast, the other major account of the voyage, *De Moluccis Indicis* by Maximilianus Transylvanus, is considered a secondary source because it was compiled from interviews with survivors rather than from direct experience.

One intriguing complication: Pigafetta's name does not appear in the official records of the *Casa de Contratación* in Seville (the colonial agency that handled overseas expeditions before 1524). Historical researcher Martín Fernández de Navarrete suggests that Pigafetta may have been listed as "Antonio Lombardo," referring to his origin from a region adjacent to his birthplace in Vicenza.

**The Manuscripts**

Four known manuscript versions of Pigafetta's account survive:
- One in Italian, held at the Ambrosiana Library in Milan
- Three in French, the earliest of which (*Le Voyage et Navigation*) dates to around 1525 in Paris

The French version became the basis for subsequent translations into multiple languages. According to Giovanni Battista Ramusio's account of the tradition, Pigafetta first wrote a brief Italian summary that he presented to Louise de Savoy (mother of King Francis I of France), and later composed a more complete version.

**Highlights of the Account**

Magellan's fleet, five ships (*Victoria*, *Concepción*, *San Antonio*, *Santiago*, *Trinidad*) and approximately 270 crew, left Seville on August 10, 1519, beginning the actual voyage on September 20.

Key events as narrated by Pigafetta:

- After crossing the Atlantic and reaching South America, a mutiny by discontented Spanish captains was quickly suppressed.
- On October 21, 1520, Magellan discovered a passage between the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans, now called the **Strait of Magellan**. Only three of the five ships made it through (the *San Antonio* deserted; the *Santiago* was wrecked).
- Crossing the Pacific brought immense suffering. The crew was reduced to eating leather, sawdust, and mice to survive.
- On March 6, 1521, they reached Guam (initially called the Island of Sails, then *Isla de Ladrones* after natives stole a boat). *Ladrones* is Spanish for thieves.
- On **March 16, 1521**, they landed on the island of Zamal (Samar), marking the first European contact with the Philippine archipelago. Magellan named the islands the *Archipelago of St. Lazarus* because the date coincided with the feast day.
- Proceeding to Homonhon, they encountered Rajah Siagun and Rajah Colambu of Butuan and Caraga.
- On March 27–28, they reached Masao (Mazaua?) in Butuan, where Magellan and Rajah Colambu performed a **blood compact**, a traditional ritual of alliance. On March 31, Mass was offered and a cross was erected.
- With Colambu's assistance, the expedition sailed to **Cebu**, arriving April 7, 1521, where they met Rajah Humabon. Friendship was again sealed through a blood compact. On April 14, a Mass was held; over 800 natives including Humabon and his wife were baptized. Humabon received the Christian name Fernando; his wife, Juana. The **Santo Niño** (image of the Child Jesus) was presented as a gift to Juana, an image still venerated in Cebu today.
- Not all chiefs welcomed the Spanish. **Lapu-Lapu** of Mactan refused to submit. Against Humabon's advice, Magellan agreed to lead an assault on Mactan personally on April 27, 1521. He was killed in the **Battle of Mactan**, ending the expedition's initial phase.

---

#### Fray Juan de Plasencia and the Customs of the Tagalogs

The second chronicler discussed in this lesson is **Fray Juan de Plasencia**, a Franciscan friar who arrived in the Philippines in 1577. His account, *Customs of the Tagalogs* (c. 1589), provides one of the earliest and most detailed descriptions of the religious beliefs, social practices, and daily life of the Tagalog people.

Unlike Pigafetta's account, Plasencia's work reflects the **perspective of a Spanish missionary**, someone who observed the Tagalogs from a position of religious authority. This creates a significant source of bias that students must consider: Plasencia consistently frames indigenous religious practices as idolatrous, superstitious, or devilish, using colonial-Christian terminology to describe them. His purpose was partly descriptive and partly evangelical, he documented practices in order to help other missionaries understand and eventually suppress or replace them.

Despite this bias, the account is invaluable for understanding pre-colonial Tagalog society.

**Key Topics Covered in Plasencia's Account**

*On their religious beliefs:*

The Tagalogs recognized a supreme deity they called **Bathala** ("the maker of all things"), who dwelled in heaven but governed all things. They also venerated the moon (especially during its new phase), certain stars (including the morning star, called *Tala*, and the Pleiades, called *Mapolon* and *Balatic*), and various idols called *lic-ha*. They also paid reverence to crocodiles (*buaya*) out of fear of being harmed.

*On superstitious beliefs:*

The Tagalogs observed many omens. Encountering a specific bird called *Tigmamanuguin* singing in a tree, a serpent, a rat, or hearing someone sneeze while leaving the house were considered bad omens that required one to return home and wait. They also practiced divination to determine the fortune of weapons like daggers.

*On rites of passage:*

During a girl's first menstrual period, she was blindfolded for four days and four nights while relatives gathered for a feast. After this period, a ritual figure called the *catalonan* bathed her, washed her head, and removed the blindfold, reportedly to bring her good fortune in marriage and fertility.

*On the spirit world:*

The Tagalogs recognized supernatural beings including:
- *Vibit* (ghosts) and *tigbalaang* (phantoms)
- *Patianac*, the spirit of a woman who died in childbirth, believed to wander and lament at night

*On time and seasons:*

The Tagalogs did not follow a fixed calendar. Instead, they tracked time through agricultural cycles, lunar phases, and the changing patterns of trees (flowers, fruit, leaf cycles). They distinguished only between the dry season (*sun-time*) and the wet season (*water-time*). Plasencia noted that after conversion to Christianity, the Spaniards introduced the European calendar, including months and weeks.

*On sacrifices and rituals:*

Ritual offerings were made before idols, involving animals (goats, fowls, swine), rice, and food items including *buyo* (betel). The officiating figure, called a *catalonan* (who could be male or female), performed sacred songs while participants made responses. The purpose of these sacrifices ranged from healing the sick and ensuring safe voyages to securing good harvests and successful marriages.

*On religious specialists (Priests of the Devil):*

Plasencia catalogued twelve types of religious practitioners, though the term "priest of the devil" reflects his colonial-Christian framing rather than how these roles were understood within Tagalog culture itself. The twelve roles included:

| Name | Role |
|---|---|
| *Catalonan* | The chief religious officiator; male or female; held in high esteem |
| *Mangagauay* | A healer-witch capable of inducing and curing illness |
| *Manyisalat* | Specialist in matters of love, capable of causing separation between couples |
| *Mancocolam* | One believed to emit fire from themselves at night |
| *Hocloban* | A more powerful witch, said to kill with a glance |
| *Silagan* | Found in Catanduanes; believed to eat human livers |
| *Magtatangal* | Said to walk at night without a head or entrails |
| *Osuang* | Sorcerer among the Visayans, said to fly and eat human flesh |
| *Mangagayoma* | Made love charms from herbs, stones, and wood |
| *Sonat* | A kind of bishop figure who assisted the dying and predicted the fate of souls |
| *Pangatahojan* | Soothsayer, predictor of the future |
| *Bayoguin* | A person whose nature inclined toward that of the opposite gender |

*On beliefs about death and burial:*

The deceased was buried beside their house. For chieftains, a small structure was built over the grave. Before burial, the dead were mourned for four days, then laid in a boat used as a coffin or bier. Animals placed inside the boat were cared for by a slave. If the deceased was a warrior, a living slave was bound beneath the body until death. Relatives mourned through singing, accompanied by eating and drinking.

The Tagalogs believed in an afterlife: *maca* (a place of rest, like paradise) for the just and valiant, and *casamaan* (a place of punishment and grief) for the wicked. Only Bathala resided in the highest heaven.

Plasencia concluded his account with the observation that, following Christianization, these practices had entirely disappeared, framed as an unambiguous success of Spanish missionary activity.

### Lesson 2: Kartilya ng Katipunan

#### Context: The Founding of the Katipunan

The Katipunan was founded on July 7, 1892, by Andres Bonifacio and a small group of Manilans, motivated by the failure of the peaceful Reform Movement and the disbanding of José Rizal's La Liga Filipina. In need of an organization that could pursue independence through direct action, Bonifacio built the Katipunan as a mass revolutionary society open to ordinary Filipinos regardless of class.

To establish order and a shared moral foundation among its members, the Katipunan produced a foundational document known as the **Kartilya ng Katipunan**, a set of principles, oaths, and teachings governing the conduct of all members.

---

#### Emilio Jacinto: The Author

The Kartilya is almost universally attributed to **Emilio Jacinto** (c. 1875–1899), who served as Bonifacio's closest intellectual adviser and is often called the "Brain of the Katipunan."

Despite never completing a formal university degree, Jacinto was intellectually gifted. He rose rapidly within the Katipunan, serving as intelligence director in the assault on San Juan del Monte on August 30, 1896. He also authored a collection of political and social essays titled *Liwanag at Dilim* (Light and Darkness), which included pieces such as *Sa Anak ng Bayan* (To the Children of the Nation) and *Ang Tao'y Magkakapantay* (All People Are Equal).

In the hills of Majayjay, Laguna, where he had set up a secret headquarters, Jacinto contracted malaria. He died on April 6, 1899, at the age of approximately 24.

---

#### The Kartilya: Content and Significance

The Kartilya is a primary source containing the oaths and guiding principles that Katipunan members were required to follow. According to British scholar Jim Richardson (2013), the Kartilya was sold at four *kualta* per copy, though it is unclear whether individual members or their chapters paid for it. Evidence suggests the document continued to circulate even after the revolution shifted from opposing Spain to resisting American control, as a version of it appears in the Philippine Insurgent Records bearing the seal of General Artemio Ricarte from 1899.

Richardson distinguishes the Kartilya from Bonifacio's own *Decalogue* (which enumerated the duties of Katipunan members): Jacinto's Kartilya, he argues, was more expansive, focusing on **aspirations and moral values**, the kind of person a Katipunero should aspire to be, rather than simply listing obligations.

The Kartilya is believed to have been influenced partly by declarations used by Masonic lodges in Manila, including ideals about the equality of men and women. The document emphasized discipline, virtue, and moral integrity. Members who violated its teachings were subject to punishment scaled to the seriousness of their offense.

The key principles in the Kartilya stress:
- Love of country as a supreme obligation
- The dignity and equality of all human beings
- Rejection of cruelty, dishonesty, and exploitation
- Reverence for women
- Duty to aid others, especially the poor and oppressed
- Prioritizing collective welfare over personal interest

### Lesson 3: The Declaration of Philippine Independence

#### Historical Background

The **Philippine Declaration of Independence** was proclaimed by Emilio Aguinaldo on **June 12, 1898**, at his ancestral house in Kawit, Cavite. This proclamation came in the context of the Spanish-American War, during which U.S. naval forces under Commodore George Dewey had destroyed the Spanish fleet in Manila Bay on May 1, 1898. With Spain's naval power collapsed, the Filipino revolutionary forces, who had been in exile in Hong Kong following the Pact of Biak-na-Bato, returned to resume the revolution.

Before the proclamation, Aguinaldo had reorganized the revolutionary government. Through a decree on June 18, 1898, he established a dictatorial government and conferred on himself broad executive powers, including the right to issue pardons, amnesty, and adopt an official flag. The dictatorial government preceded and enabled the formal independence declaration.

The declaration document, known formally as the **Acta de la Proclamación de la Independencia del Pueblo Filipino**, was written and publicly read by **Ambrocio Rianzares Bautista** before a crowd of thousands from various provinces. At the same occasion:
- The Philippine flag, sewn in Hong Kong, was raised and waved publicly for the first time
- The national anthem was performed for the first time by composer **Julian Felipe**, who was instructed that it should sound like a triumphant march, express gratitude for Spain's contributions, and symbolize the country's love of freedom

---

#### Points of Historical Interest and Controversy

The Declaration carries several historically significant features and contested points:

1. **Aguinaldo's missing signature.** Of the 98 signatories of the declaration, Aguinaldo's signature does not appear (Ocampo, 2020). The reasons for this remain a subject of historical discussion.

2. **American presence.** Commodore Dewey was invited to attend the proclamation but did not appear. An American military officer, Colonel L.M. Johnson, was among the signatories, a fact that raises questions about the declaration's significance to U.S. authorities.

3. **Apolinario Mabini's opposition.** The most capable political mind of the revolutionary government, Mabini (who arrived that day and met Aguinaldo for the first time), considered the proclamation "reckless and premature." He believed the Philippines was not yet ready, it needed more weapons and ammunition before announcing independence.

4. **Absence of immediate ratification.** The declaration was not immediately promulgated into law or recognized internationally, adding to the debate about the legal standing of the event.

---

#### Who Wrote the Declaration: Ambrocio Rianzares Bautista

Born in Biñan, Laguna in 1830, Rianzares Bautista (known as "Don Bosyong") held a degree in law from the University of Santo Tomas. He was a lawyer and political figure who had served as an officer of Rizal's La Liga Filipina. During the revolution, he was among the most wanted by Spanish authorities for his participation in underground movements. He was imprisoned in Fort Santiago, released, and later granted a general amnesty under the Pact of Biak-na-Bato.

When Aguinaldo returned from exile in May 1898, Bautista became his political adviser. He convinced Aguinaldo to establish a dictatorial, rather than a constitutional, government, arguing it better suited the urgency of the times. After independence was proclaimed, however, Mabini replaced Bautista as Aguinaldo's primary adviser.

### Lesson 4: The Malolos Constitution and the First Philippine Republic

#### From Revolution to Republic

The proclamation of independence on June 12, 1898 set in motion a process of political organization. Aguinaldo issued decrees reorganizing the government in liberated provinces and calling for the election of delegates to a Revolutionary Congress to draft a constitution.

Before the convention, the political landscape had already been shaped by important events:

- **The Tejeros Convention (March 22, 1897)**, attempted to reconcile the Magdiwang and Magdalo factions by dissolving the Katipunan and establishing a new revolutionary government
- **The Pact of Biak-na-Bato (December 15, 1897)**, ended the first phase of the revolution; Aguinaldo and key revolutionaries went into exile in Hong Kong in exchange for financial indemnities and pardons
- **Return from exile (May 1898)**, following the Spanish-American War, Aguinaldo returned to resume the revolution

On **September 15, 1898**, the Revolutionary Congress convened at the **Church of Barasoain** in Malolos, Bulacan to draft the constitution. This body came to be known as the **Malolos Congress**, composed of both elected and appointed delegates from across the archipelago.

---

#### The Malolos Constitution

After revisions and debate, Aguinaldo promulgated the **Malolos Constitution** on **January 21, 1899**.

Key features:

- It established **three branches of government**: Executive, Legislative, and Judiciary
- It was composed of **93 articles** divided into **14 titles**, plus 8 articles of transitory provisions and one additional unnumbered article
- It is historically significant as the **first Philippine constitution** and is recognized as the **first republican constitution enacted in Asia**
- Congress elected Aguinaldo as **President of the Philippines**

On **January 23, 1899**, the **First Republic of the Philippines** was inaugurated in Malolos, marking the formal start of Aguinaldo's presidency and the beginning of what became known as the First Philippine Republic.

### Lesson 5: Visual Sources in the Study of History

#### What Are Visual Sources?

History is not recorded only in written texts. Visual sources, photographs, paintings, drawings, maps, and political cartoons, are also primary sources that reveal how people saw, experienced, and represented the world around them.

Like any primary source, visual sources carry the biases and perspectives of their creators. Reading them critically requires asking: *Who created this? For what audience? With what purpose?*

---

#### Political Cartoons as Historical Sources: Alfred McCoy and Alfredo Roces

**Political cartoons** are illustrations that translate editorial positions into satirical, sarcastic, or burlesque images. They reflect public perception and are powerful indicators of the social climate, especially regarding press freedom.

During the Spanish Period, press censorship suppressed most political commentary. This changed under American rule, when the colonial government promoted a "free press" as part of its broader political project. The early 20th century saw a flourishing of local newspapers and magazines that became increasingly bold in commenting on Philippine political life, corruption, and the colonial relationship.

The book *Political Cartoons: Political Caricature of the American Era, 1901–1941* (1985), authored by Alfred McCoy and Alfredo Roces, documents this visual history. It collects cartoons from local publications and analyzes them as evidence of political culture during the American period.

**Key themes depicted in these cartoons include:**

- **Unequal pay**, Cartoons from *The Independent* (1915) and *Bag-ong Kusog* (1928) criticized the salary gap between American and Filipino workers doing equivalent jobs, with Americans paid far more for similar or less demanding roles.

- **Power dynamics between colonizer and colonized**, A cartoon from *Lipag Kalabaw* (November 14, 1908) depicted Juan dela Cruz (representing the Filipino) questioning Uncle Sam (representing the United States) about restrictions on freedom of speech. The relative size and posture of the figures, Juan standing and enduring, Sam comfortably seated, visually communicated the colonial power imbalance.

- **Anti-friar sentiment**, Even as Spanish influence waned politically, cartoonists mercilessly satirized the continued political power of the Catholic friars and alleged hypocrisy of Church ministers.

- **Filipino politicians and corruption**, Manila's political class was repeatedly depicted as self-serving, corrupt, and abusive. The city itself was shown as a haven for political dynasties and graft.

- **Anti-Chinese and later anti-Japanese sentiment**, As economic conditions fluctuated, cartoonists turned their critical attention toward Chinese merchants (particularly during the rice crisis of 1919) and later toward Japan's expanding military ambitions in East Asia.

---

#### Juan Luna and the Spoliarium

**Juan Luna** (1857–1899) was born in Badoc, Ilocos Norte and is remembered as one of the Philippines' greatest painters. He trained under Spanish painter Agustín Sáez and at the Academia de Dibujo y Pintura under Filipino painter Lorenzo Guerrero before traveling to Spain for further study at the Real Academia de Bellas Artes de San Fernando. His work won gold medals at the Barcelona Exposition (1888) and the Universal Exposition of Fine Arts in Madrid (1884).

His most celebrated masterpiece, the **Spoliarium**, depicts the aftermath of gladiatorial combat in ancient Rome, specifically, the *spoliarium*, an underground chamber beneath the Colosseum where fallen gladiators were stripped of their weapons and armor before disposal. The painting measures 4.22 × 7.675 meters and is the largest painting in the Philippines.

Technically, the Spoliarium is notable for its dramatic use of **chiaroscuro**, the contrasting of light and dark, which highlights mangled bodies in the center of the composition against a dark, oppressive background.

Allegorically, José Rizal interpreted the Spoliarium as a representation of the condition of the Philippines under colonial rule, humanity unredeemed, idealism in struggle against injustice. In his tribute to Luna, Rizal declared that "genius knows no country; genius sprouts everywhere."

---

#### Fernando Amorsolo: Landscape and War

**Fernando Amorsolo** (1892–1972) was Manila-born and trained at the Liceo de Manila and the School of Fine Arts of the University of the Philippines, from which he graduated with honors in 1914. He later studied at the Academia de San Fernando in Madrid and worked in New York, where he was exposed to cubism and impressionism.

He was named National Artist in Painting in 1972.

During World War II, Amorsolo shifted from his signature luminous landscapes of rural Filipino life to witnessing and painting the destruction brought by war:

- **The Bombing of the Intendencia**, painted from his house as he observed the burning of the colonial customs building, capturing the destruction and deaths caused by the fighting
- **The Burning of Manila**, depicts the widespread destruction of the capital during the Japanese occupation

His earlier painting **Planting Rice** (1951) became iconic, a luminous depiction of women working in ricefields that became a model for subsequent generations of Philippine painters.

### Lesson 1: The Site of the First Mass

#### The Controversy

One of the most debated questions in Philippine colonial history is: *Where did the first Easter Mass on Philippine soil take place?* The two main competing claims are **Limasawa** (Southern Leyte) and **Butuan** (Agusan del Norte). The controversy matters because the site of the first Mass is considered the site of the first Christian celebration in the Philippines, and potentially in Southeast Asia.

The debate is based on **Pigafetta's account** of the Magellan expedition, specifically his identification of a location he called "Mazaua" or "Masao", the place where Magellan's crew first heard Mass in the Philippines on March 31, 1521.

---

#### The Historical Backdrop

The journey in question followed Magellan's first contact with the Philippine archipelago on March 16, 1521 (Samar). The crew made contact with Rajah Colambu in Homonhon, then, assisted by Colambu, sailed to the location identified in the account as Mazaua, arriving March 28–29 and departing April 4. It was at Mazaua that Magellan and Rajah Colambu performed a blood compact and where Fr. Pedro de Valderrama offered Mass on March 31.

The precise geographic identity of Mazaua has been contested by historians for over a century.

---

#### The Competing Claims

**The Limasawa Position:**
Proponents argue that Mazaua refers to Limasawa, a small island in Southern Leyte. This was the position of the National Historical Commission of the Philippines (NHCP) for most of the 20th century. Its supporters cite navigational and geographical evidence from Pigafetta's account that aligns with Limasawa's location, currents, and visible landmarks.

**The Butuan/Masao Position:**
Proponents argue that Mazaua refers to Masao in Butuan, Agusan del Norte (historically part of the old territory of Butuan). They point to the explicit mention of Butuan in Pigafetta's account and to the coordinates Pigafetta recorded (approximately 9 degrees 40 minutes North latitude), which they argue places the location more consistent with Butuan's latitude.

---

#### Key Investigations and Panels

**The Gancayco Committee (1979):**
A National Committee was organized and its report, known as the Gancayco Report, concluded in favor of Limasawa based on assessment of the sailing routes, land forms, river deltas, and geographical location by latitude as described in primary sources. This report was used as a basis by the NHCP for decades.

**The Mojares Panel (2018–2019):**
In preparation for the 500th anniversary of the arrival of Christianity in the Philippines, the National Historical Commission of the Philippines and the National Quincentennial Committee created a new panel to reexamine the question. Chaired by Dr. Resil Mojares and including scholars from several universities and the Catholic Bishops' Conference of the Philippines, the panel conducted evaluation proceedings in December 2018 in Cebu City.

Two respondents before the panel, Gabriel Atega and Dr. Potenciano Malvar, both argued in favor of Butuan but from different directions. Interestingly, both agreed that the site of the Mass was likely a *different* location from Mazaua itself, and both pointed to an 1872 monument in Magallanes, Agusan del Norte as potentially identifying the actual site of the mass. Both also noted that from the heights near that monument, Camiguin Island appears as three visible "mountains" to the west-southwest, which aligns with accounts of three islands visible from the location of the cross-planting.

The controversy remains unresolved at the scholarly level and continues to be a subject of active historical debate.

### Lesson 2: The Cavite Mutiny of 1872

#### Overview

The **Cavite Mutiny** (in Spanish sources, *La Algarada Caviteña*) was a brief uprising of Filipino soldiers and laborers serving the Spanish armed forces at **Fort San Felipe** in Cavite Province on January 20, 1872. The mutineers believed their action could spark a wider national uprising, but the Spanish military crushed it within hours.

Although the mutiny itself was small in scale, its historical significance lies in **how it was used** by Governor-General Rafael de Izquierdo. Izquierdo exploited the uprising as a pretext to crack down on Filipinos who had enjoyed the relatively liberal atmosphere under his predecessor, Governor-General Carlos María de la Torre. A wave of arrests targeted reform-minded individuals, including secular (Filipino) priests campaigning for ecclesiastical rights.

The most consequential result was the arrest, trial, and public execution on **February 17, 1872** of three priests who had been prominent in the movement for secularization, the transfer of parishes from Spanish friars to Filipino secular clergy:

- **Fr. Mariano Gómez** (age 73)
- **Fr. José Burgos** (age 35)
- **Fr. Jacinto Zamora** (age 37)

The three, known collectively as **GOMBURZA**, were executed by garrote at Bagumbayan (now Luneta). Many were deported to the Marianas and Caroline Islands.

The execution had profound historical consequences: it radicalized young Filipinos, including José Rizal, who dedicated his novel *El Filibusterismo* to their memory. GOMBURZA are remembered as martyrs of Filipino nationalism.

---

#### Three Versions of the Events

The module introduces this lesson through **three surviving accounts** of the Cavite Mutiny, which demonstrate sharply different perspectives depending on the author's nationality, position, and agenda. All three were translated into English and published by historian Gregorio Zaide (1990) in *Documentary Sources of Philippine History*.

**1. The Spanish Version, José Montero y Vidal**

Montero y Vidal was a Spanish government official residing in Manila and the author of major works on Philippine colonial history. According to historian Gregorio Zaide, his account of the Cavite Mutiny is heavily biased against Filipinos and the Jesuits and largely supports the official position of Governor-General Izquierdo. American historian James LeRoy observed that Montero y Vidal became notably "rabid" in his treatment of the Cavite events, strongly condemning the executed priests and their associates as guilty of separatist ambitions, and mocking alternative accounts. Despite its clear bias, Montero y Vidal's account remains one of the few surviving Spanish accounts of the uprising.

### Lesson 1: The Constitutions of the Philippines

#### A History of Philippine Constitutions

The Philippines has had multiple constitutions, each reflecting the political circumstances of its time:

**The Malolos Constitution (1899)**, The first Philippine constitution, drafted by the Malolos Congress and promulgated on January 21, 1899. It established the First Philippine Republic, provided for three branches of government, and is historically celebrated as the first republican constitution in Asia. The republic it founded was short-lived: the Philippine-American War (1899–1902) ended Filipino sovereignty.

**The 1935 Commonwealth Constitution**

Following decades of American colonial administration, the Philippine Commonwealth was established under the Tydings-McDuffie Act (1934), which provided for a 10-year transition toward independence. The 1935 Constitution was ratified by plebiscite and established the government of the Commonwealth. It provided for a strong executive, a bicameral legislature (Senate and House of Representatives), and an independent judiciary. Manuel Quezon became the first Commonwealth President.

Under the 1935 Constitution, the Philippines was to become fully independent on July 4, 1946, a plan interrupted by World War II and the Japanese occupation.

**The 1943 Japanese-Sponsored Constitution**

During the Japanese occupation (1941–1945), a puppet Philippine Republic was established by the Japanese colonial government. The 1943 Constitution governed this republic under Jose P. Laurel. This constitution was generally dismissed as illegitimate by Filipino resistance movements and allied nations, as it was imposed by an occupying foreign power.

**The 1973 Constitution (Marcos Period)**

Following the declaration of Martial Law by President Ferdinand Marcos on September 21, 1972, a new constitution was ratified under controversial circumstances in January 1973. Critics argued that it was ratified through staged Citizens' Assemblies rather than a genuine democratic process. The 1973 Constitution concentrated power in the executive and effectively allowed Marcos to rule by decree without legislative oversight. It remains controversial as a product of authoritarian governance.

**The 1987 Constitution**

The current constitution of the Philippines was drafted by a Constitutional Commission following the 1986 People Power Revolution, which ousted Marcos and brought Corazon Aquino to power. Ratified on February 2, 1987, it restored democratic governance, reestablished the Senate, and introduced significant protections for civil liberties and human rights.

A distinguishing feature of the 1987 Constitution is its extensive treatment of **social justice** as a core constitutional value, a direct response to the social inequalities reinforced during the colonial period and the Marcos years. As Constitutional Commission President Justice Cecilia Muñoz-Palma stated: "Social justice is the heart of the 1987 Constitution."

Social justice, as defined in the landmark *Calalang v. Williams* case by Justice José Laurel, means the humanization of laws and the equalization of social and economic forces so that justice may be approximated for all, not a radical ideology but a commitment to the state's role in promoting the welfare of all people.

### Lesson 2: The Indigenous Peoples of the Philippines

#### Who Are the Indigenous Peoples?

The term **Indigenous Peoples (IPs)** refers broadly to communities whose historical continuity connects them to the pre-colonial era of their territories. The most widely cited definition draws from the United Nations, based on the study by José Martínez Cobo (1983), which identifies historical continuity through:

1. Occupation of ancestral lands (or part of them)
2. Common ancestry with the original occupants of those lands
3. Distinct cultural expressions (religion, tribal systems, dress, livelihood, lifestyle)
4. Language (as primary, maternal, or habitual means of communication)
5. Residence in particular regions
6. Other relevant factors

The Philippines was among the first countries in Asia to formally adopt the term "Indigenous Peoples" in policy language. The country is home to approximately 110 ethno-linguistic groups comprising an estimated 14–17 million people.

**Distribution of Indigenous Peoples in the Philippines:**
- **Mindanao:** approximately 61% of the IP population; includes the Lumad (18 non-Muslim IP groups; *Lumad* is a Visayan word meaning "of the land") and the Moro Peoples (seven Islamized groups)
- **Northern Luzon (Cordillera Administrative Region):** approximately 33%; includes the Igorot peoples (Ifugao, Ibaloy, Bontoc, Isneg, Tinguia, Kankanaey, the term *Igorot* meaning "people from the mountain")
- **Visayas and other regions:** the remaining percentage, including the Mangyan of Mindoro (eight sub-groups)

---

#### Historical Context: Colonialism and Classification

During the Spanish colonial period, communities that resisted conversion to Christianity and colonial authority were labeled *infieles* (infidels) or *salvajes* (savages). Under American rule, these groups were reclassified as "non-Christian tribes." In 1901, the Bureau of Non-Christian Tribes was established and the anthropologist David Barrows supervised the 1903 Philippine Census, which classified Filipinos along religious lines into "Christian and Civilized Tribes" and "Non-Christian and Wild Tribes."

This history of classification reveals how colonial governments used categories to manage and marginalize peoples who fell outside their preferred social order. Despite these pressures, many IP communities successfully preserved their distinct cultures, languages, and governance systems.

IPs are among the most disadvantaged groups in the Philippines today, facing issues of land rights, access to education, healthcare, and political representation.

---

#### Legal Protections: The IPRA and the 1987 Constitution

The **1987 Constitution** explicitly recognizes indigenous peoples' rights:

> "The State recognizes and promotes the rights of indigenous cultural communities within the framework of national unity and development.", Article II, Section 22

In 1997, under President Fidel Ramos, the **Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act (IPRA)**, also known as Republic Act 8371, was enacted. IPRA established four bundles of rights for IPs:

1. **Right to Ancestral Domains and Lands**
2. **Right to Self-Governance and Empowerment**
3. **Right to Social Justice and Human Rights**
4. **Right to Cultural Integrity**

In 1998, the **National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP)** was created to implement IPRA and protect the interests, welfare, customs, and institutions of indigenous communities.

### Lesson 3: History of Agrarian Reform in the Philippines

#### What Is Agrarian Reform?

**Agrarian reform** refers broadly to the redistribution of land to landless farmers and farm workers, together with the support services needed to make such redistribution effective. The legal definition in Philippine law encompasses land redistribution, production and profit-sharing arrangements, labor management administration, and all other measures designed to improve the economic status of agricultural workers.

Agricultural workers have historically formed the backbone of the Philippine economy, yet many remained poor despite cultivating some of the country's richest land, a paradox rooted in the colonial land tenure systems imposed during the Spanish period.

---

#### Historical Development of Agrarian Reform in the Philippines

**Spanish Colonial Period:**
Under Spanish rule, the *encomienda* system and later the *hacienda* system concentrated large tracts of agricultural land in the hands of the Church, Spanish nobles, and the colonial government. Filipino farmers worked as tenants under arrangements that extracted a large portion of their harvests. This created a deeply embedded system of landlordism that would persist long after colonial rule ended.

**American Period:**
The Americans initially promised land reforms but largely maintained the hacienda structure. The *friar lands* (estates held by religious orders) were purchased by the colonial government and resold, but often to wealthy Filipinos or companies rather than to the tenant farmers working the land.

**Commonwealth and Post-War Period:**
Successive Philippine presidents introduced agrarian legislation, but implementation was consistently limited. The **Land Reform Act of 1955** and **Agricultural Land Reform Code of 1963** (Republic Act 3844) were significant attempts, but both fell short of substantive redistribution.

**Marcos Period:**
President Marcos proclaimed the "green revolution" and issued Presidential Decree 27 (1972), which mandated land transfer for rice and corn lands. While celebrated as a major reform, its implementation was uneven and limited in scope, excluding vast portions of the agricultural sector.

**CARP (1988):**
The most ambitious attempt at agrarian reform was the **Comprehensive Agrarian Reform Program (CARP)**, enacted as Republic Act 6657 in 1988 under President Corazon Aquino. CARP aimed to distribute agricultural lands of all types (not just rice and corn) to qualified beneficiaries. Despite its broad scope, implementation has been widely criticized as slow, incomplete, and subject to legal challenges by landowners.

Decades after CARP, significant numbers of Filipino farmers remain without land of their own, continuing to work under tenancy arrangements similar to those that existed under colonial rule.

### Lesson 1: Bridges in 19th-Century Philippines as Historical Sources

#### Bridges as Material Evidence

Material culture, physical objects built or modified by humans, constitutes one of the most durable forms of historical evidence. The stone bridges constructed during the Spanish colonial period in the Philippines offer a particularly rich example of how built structures can illuminate colonial history, labor relations, and cultural exchange.

Colonial-era bridges were built primarily using **cut stone** sourced from local geological materials, volcanic rock, limestone, and similar formations. The mortar used to bind the stones (*argamasa*) was made from powdered lime mixed with water. To increase durability, local materials were sometimes added to the mixture: plant sap, molasses, and egg white.

The architectural style of these bridges is described as **Arquitectura Mestiza**, "mixed architecture," a term used by the Jesuit Ignacio Alcina as early as 1668 to describe the blend of European design with Philippine materials and labor. The designs were originally European; the construction workers were primarily Filipino *polistas* (those fulfilling labor obligations under the colonial *polo y servicios* system).

---

#### What Bridges Reveal

These bridges are not merely engineering artifacts, they are documents of social and political history:

**1. Colonial labor systems:** The bridges were built using forced or tribute labor (*polistas*) drawn from local communities. The workers' identity survives in subtle ways: Masonic symbols carved beneath the structures indicate that Filipino stone cutters were members of craft societies and marked their work, asserting their presence even in anonymous colonial construction.

**2. Economic integration:** Bridges connected *pueblos* (towns) and *alcaldias* (provincial units), facilitating the movement of goods, military forces, and missionaries. They accelerated the economic integration of the colonial territory.

**3. Boundary markers:** Beyond connection, bridges also served as demarcation lines between towns and provinces, physically marking territorial jurisdiction.

**4. The builders' identity:** While bridges typically bore markers identifying the Spanish official who commissioned or oversaw construction, the actual craft was carried out by Filipino laborers whose contributions have been largely overlooked in official colonial records.

Notable examples of surviving colonial bridges include the **Puente de Malagonlong** in Tayabas (present-day Quezon Province) and the **Puente de España** in Manila.

### Lesson 2: The "Moro" Problem, Origins and Persistence

#### Understanding the Term

Before the arrival of the Spaniards in 1565, Muslims in the Philippines were among the most politically powerful groups in the archipelago. Islam had spread through Mindanao, the Sulu Archipelago, Palawan, and even parts of Luzon (including Manila, Mindoro, and Batangas) and the Visayas. Muslim rulers (sultans and datus) wielded significant political and economic authority.

The Spanish brought with them a centuries-long conflict with Muslims rooted in the Iberian Peninsula. During the **Conquista** (Muslim conquest of Spain) and the subsequent **Reconquista** (Christian reconquest of the peninsula), the Spanish developed deep antipathy toward Muslims, whom they called **Moros**, a term derived from the North African peoples ("Moors") who had invaded Spain. When they encountered Muslim communities in the Philippines, the Spaniards automatically applied this label, framing Muslim Filipinos as their natural enemies despite the fact that Philippine Muslims had no connection whatsoever to the North African Moors.

---

#### Origins of the Conflict

The mutual hostility between the Spanish colonial state and Muslim Filipino communities developed through the colonial period through several mechanisms:

- The Spaniards subjected Philippine Muslim communities to repeated military campaigns (the *Moro Wars*), attempting to break Muslim political autonomy
- Muslims were categorically labeled in colonial historiography as backward, violent, and opposed to civilization
- In response, Muslim communities launched raids against Christianized coastal settlements, particularly in the Visayas, partly for economic resources and partly as an assertion of autonomy against colonial encroachment

From the Muslim perspective, these raids were acts of self-defense against Spanish subjugation. From the Spanish perspective (and from that of Christianized Filipinos used in military expeditions against Muslim communities), they were unprovoked piracy and violence.

This cycle of raiding and retaliation, mediated by Spanish colonial framing, created a legacy of mutual suspicion and hostility that outlasted Spanish rule.

---

#### The American Period and After

When the Philippines transferred from Spanish to American sovereignty following the Spanish-American War (1898), the Moro problem was inherited rather than resolved. Through the **Bates Treaty** (1899, later reduced in scope to the Bates Agreement), the Sultan of Sulu formally recognized American sovereignty over Muslim Mindanao, though with significant reservations.

Initially resistant to American authority, many Moro leaders eventually began to prefer American rule over incorporation into a Philippine republic dominated by Christian Filipinos. A number petitioned the Americans to maintain their colonial status if independence was granted to the rest of the Philippines, a request that was not granted.

After independence in 1946, the integration of Muslim Mindanao into the Philippine republic remained deeply contested. Key developments include:

- A 1954 congressional committee recommended a commission to address Muslim integration, leading to **Republic Act 1888** and the Commission on National Integration
- A 1963 Senate Committee report identified the root causes of Muslim discontent as: (1) land problems, (2) educational disparity, (3) lack of livelihood, and (4) inadequate health and transportation infrastructure
- By the 1960s and 1970s, the Moro Problem had escalated to armed separatism, with groups like the Moro National Liberation Front (MNLF) demanding an independent Muslim homeland

Academic studies from the early 1970s found that negative perceptions of Muslims persisted even in elite Filipino institutions. A Filipinas Foundation study (1971) found that 65% of Filipino Muslims viewed their Muslim identity as more fundamental than their Filipino identity, in part because "Filipino" was perceived as synonymous with Christian or Bisaya identity. Mutual distrust between Christian and Muslim Filipinos remained a significant obstacle to national unity.

### Lesson 3: The Claveria Decree of 1849, Surnames and Colonial Public Order

#### Why Do Filipinos Have the Surnames They Do?

Among the many legacies of Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines is the system of surnames used by most Filipinos today. The co-existence of Spanish-sounding surnames (like Reyes, Santos, Mendoza) and indigenous surnames (like Dimagiba, Kahabagan, Poqui) in the same country reflects a specific historical event: the **Claveria Decree of 1849**.

---

#### Background: The Problem of Surnames

Before 1849, Filipinos had no systematic, enforced system of surnames. Many people arbitrarily adopted surnames, sometimes sharing the same surname with no familial relationship. Others changed their surnames casually. Governor-General Narciso Claveria noted that this created serious administrative and legal problems:

- Individuals with the same given name and surname were indistinguishable in legal and administrative records
- Property inheritance disputes arose because family members could not be reliably identified
- Taxation, civil registration, and census-taking were all complicated by the absence of stable family names
- Church ministers reported difficulty maintaining proper baptismal, marriage, and burial records

---

#### The Decree of November 21, 1849

On November 21, 1849, Governor-General Claveria issued a decree requiring the native population of the Philippines to adopt surnames from an officially compiled catalogue, a list of names drawn from Spanish vocabulary, place names, historical figures, and nature. The catalogue was distributed to each municipality in alphabetical order; municipalities were assigned to use a specific portion of the list to help authorities distinguish residents of different towns.

The implementation of the decree was uneven. In some areas, it was enforced with relative thoroughness; in others, resistance or administrative negligence meant that many indigenous names were retained or adopted alongside Spanish ones. The decree's purpose was as much about **administrative control** as about cultural transformation: by creating stable, trackable family identities, the colonial government improved its capacity to levy taxes, track legal obligations, and administer the population.

The lasting result is the diverse landscape of Philippine surnames visible today, a mix of Spanish-catalogue names and retained indigenous names that reflects both colonial imposition and local resistance.

### Lesson 4: The School Curriculum in the Philippines, Historical Development

#### Pre-Colonial Education

Before Spanish colonization, education in the Philippines was informal and community-based. Literacy in the indigenous **Baybayin** script existed among the Tagalogs and other groups. Children were taught practical skills, farming, fishing, weaving, navigation, and trade, by their families and communities. Community elders passed down oral literature, customary law, and cultural traditions.

---

#### Education Under Spanish Colonial Rule

The Spanish colonial education system was primarily an instrument of **religious conversion and social control** rather than broad civic education. The Church controlled most educational institutions; primary goals were teaching the Catholic faith and producing clerically aligned literate individuals.

Spanish was the language of instruction in higher institutions. Indigenous Baybayin literacy was largely displaced. Some schools were established for indigenous children (*escuelas de primeras letras*), though access was uneven and quality varied widely.

José Rizal's own educational trajectory illustrates the opportunities and constraints of the period. He studied at the Ateneo Municipal in Manila, then at the University of Santo Tomas (UST), where he was exposed to Spanish classical education, literature, philosophy, sciences. His dissatisfaction with the university's scholastic curriculum led him to pursue further education in Europe.

---

#### Education Under American Rule

The American colonial system introduced one of the most dramatic educational transformations in Philippine history. Beginning in 1901, the colonial government deployed over a thousand American teachers (popularly known as the **Thomasites**, after the ship *Thomas* that brought many of them) to establish a public school system modeled on American education.

Key features of American colonial education:

- **English as the medium of instruction**, English displaced Filipino languages in the classroom, a decision with profound and lasting effects on Philippine society
- **Mass public education**, The colonial government built thousands of schools across the archipelago, dramatically expanding access to formal education
- **Secular curriculum**, Unlike the Spanish system, American colonial education was not Church-controlled; civics, science, and practical skills received more emphasis
- **Americanization**, The curriculum promoted American values, institutions, and cultural norms, reinforcing the Philippines' new colonial relationship with the United States

The public school system established under American rule formed the foundation of the contemporary Philippine educational structure.

---

#### Post-Independence and Contemporary Curriculum

After independence in 1946, the Philippines inherited the American-designed educational system and gradually adapted it. Key developments included:

- Introduction of Filipino (based on Tagalog/Pilipino) as a co-medium of instruction alongside English
- Periodic curriculum revisions responding to national development priorities
- Under the Marcos period, the curriculum was used to promote the ideological program of the "New Society"
- After 1986, curriculum reforms emphasized human rights, democracy, and national identity
- The **K-12 program** (implemented 2012–2016) added two years to the secondary school cycle, bringing the Philippines into alignment with international standards

Philippine education today continues to grapple with questions of language policy, access and equity, quality, and the challenge of developing an educational system that is both internationally competitive and rooted in Filipino identity and context.

### Lesson 1: Dealing with Local History

#### What Is Local History?

**Local history** is the "unheard history" of communities and localities, the collective experiences and testimonies of ordinary people, places, and institutions that conventional national history has often overlooked. Local history decentralizes historical inquiry: instead of starting from the centers of political power (capitals, rulers, major figures), it begins from the ground up, from the lives and events of communities, towns, and regions.

This is distinct from **oral history**, though related to it. Oral history refers specifically to the transmission of historical experience through spoken word across generations, the verbal testimonies of ordinary people that are often undocumented or underrepresented in official records. Oral history is frequently an important *method* within local history research.

---

#### Why Local History Matters

For much of the history of historical writing in the Philippines, scholars focused predominantly on the national narrative centered on Manila, on prominent political figures, and on major events. Regional and community experiences, the Cebuanos, the Igorots, the Visayans, the farmers and fishermen, received relatively little attention. This created significant gaps in national historical knowledge.

The growing field of local history helps to:

1. **Fill the gaps**, Illuminate events, communities, and experiences absent from the national narrative
2. **Correct imbalances**, Provide historical voice to groups marginalized in conventional accounts
3. **Strengthen local identity**, Help communities understand their own roots and contributions to the larger national story
4. **Support national development**, As local governments gained greater autonomy under the Local Government Code, local history provides the cultural and historical context for communities to understand and shape their own development

---

#### Standards for Writing Local History

Local history, like all historical writing, must meet scholarly standards:

1. **Authentic**, Original in approach; not a duplication of existing work
2. **Accurate**, Correct in factual details
3. **Objective**, As impartial as possible; avoid excessive use of subjective adjectives and superlatives
4. **Reliable**, Supported by properly authenticated and credible sources
5. **Relevant**, Addresses questions or gaps of genuine interest and value
6. **Systematic/Scientific**, Follows established research methods; applies external and internal criticism to all sources

---

#### Sources for Local History

Local historians draw on a wider range of sources than conventional historians often do:

**Written sources:**
- Archival materials (national, religious congregational, and private archives)
- Documents from the National Archives of the Philippines (classified in bundles as *Obras Publicas*, *Ereccion de Pueblos*, *Fincas*, *Testamentos*, *Provincias*, etc.)
- Correspondence, diaries, and family records
- Local newspapers and periodicals

**Tangible (non-documentary) sources:**
- Archaeological finds: tools, pottery, agricultural implements, ornaments
- Old dwellings, buildings, caves, natural landmarks
- Personal possessions with historical significance (antique objects, religious items, clothing)

**Challenges specific to Philippine local history:**
- Many primary Spanish-era documents are written in Spanish
- Documents from the colonial period are often in poor physical condition
- Archival research is time-consuming and requires travel
- Many potential sources remain unexamined in archives

### Lesson 2: Philippine Heritage and History

#### What Is Heritage?

**Heritage** is broadly defined as "something that has been passed down or inherited from ancestors or previous generations." It carries the idea of transmission, something from the past that has survived into the present and continues to carry meaning.

**Culture**, in the simplest terms, describes the shared practices, beliefs, customs, and expressions of a society, the accumulated ways a community lives and makes meaning. Heritage is culture that has proven durable enough to transcend the period of its creation and remain meaningful to later generations.

The **United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO)**, established in 1945, plays a central international role in identifying, protecting, and preserving heritage. UNESCO classifies heritage into three main categories:

---

#### UNESCO's Three Categories of Heritage

**1. Cultural Heritage**
Has two types:
- *Tangible Cultural Heritage*, Physical, material objects and sites:
  - **Movable:** Paintings, artifacts, coins, manuscripts
  - **Immovable:** Monuments, historic buildings, archaeological sites, sacred landscapes
  - **Underwater:** Shipwrecks, submerged ruins
- *Intangible Cultural Heritage*, Living practices and expressions passed from generation to generation: oral traditions, performing arts, rituals, indigenous knowledge, craftsmanship

**2. Natural Heritage**
Natural features and formations of outstanding physical, biological, or geological value: landscapes, geological formations, habitats of threatened species

**3. Heritage in Armed Conflict**
The protection of cultural property in situations of armed conflict, governed by international conventions

---

#### What History Does for Heritage

Heritage and history are not the same thing, though they are deeply connected. History provides **context**, it situates heritage in time, explains its creation, documents its significance, and reveals the human stories behind material objects. Without historical context, a heritage site is simply an old structure; with it, it becomes a readable document of the past.

A useful example is **The Ruins** in Bacolod City, the skeletal remains of an early 20th-century mansion built by Don Mariano Ledesma Lacson for his wife. During World War II, Filipino guerrillas burned the structure to prevent Japanese forces from using it as a garrison. Without knowing this history, visitors see only a picturesque ruin. With it, the site becomes a layered story of love, loss, war, and resistance.

---

#### The Heritage Cycle

Simon Thurley developed a model called the **Heritage Cycle** to describe how communities engage with heritage:

Understanding heritage → Valuing it → Taking care of it → Finding meaning in it → Understanding heritage (cycling back)

The cycle emphasizes participation: as people come to understand and find meaning in heritage, they become invested in its preservation and protection.

---

#### Philippine Heritage Law: Republic Act 10066

The **National Cultural Heritage Act of 2009** (Republic Act 10066) provides the legal framework for heritage preservation in the Philippines. It builds on the mandate of the **1987 Constitution** (Article XIV, Sections 14–17), which obligates the state to:

- Foster the preservation, enrichment, and evolution of Filipino culture
- Conserve, develop, promote, and popularize the nation's historical and cultural heritage
- Declare that the country's artistic and historic wealth constitutes "the cultural treasure of the nation" and is under state protection

The objectives of Republic Act 10066 (Article 1, Section 2) are:

(a) Protect, preserve, conserve and promote the nation's cultural heritage, its property and histories, and the ethnicity of local communities;
(b) Establish and strengthen cultural institutions; and
(c) Protect cultural workers and ensure their professional development and well-being.

Key cultural agencies involved in heritage management in the Philippines include the National Historical Commission of the Philippines (NHCP), the National Museum of the Philippines, the National Library, the Cultural Center of the Philippines (CCP), and the National Archives, among others.
